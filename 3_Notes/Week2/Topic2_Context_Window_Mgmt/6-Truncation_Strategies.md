# Truncation Strategies: Managing Context When Space Runs Out

## The Core Problem

When you work with language models in production, you will constantly face a fundamental constraint: context windows have hard limits. A conversation that starts with a few hundred tokens can grow to tens or hundreds of thousands of tokens over time as messages accumulate. A document you need to process might be larger than the entire context window. Retrieved content from a RAG system might return more information than you can fit alongside the user's query and system instructions. When you hit these limits, you must make a choice about what to keep and what to discard. This is where truncation strategies come into play.

Truncation is not just a technical necessity forced on you by context limits. It is also a design decision that fundamentally shapes how your application behaves, what it can remember, and what trade-offs you are making between recency and completeness. Understanding the different approaches to truncation and their implications is essential for building reliable systems that handle long conversations or large documents without breaking in subtle and frustrating ways.

## Truncate From Start: Prioritizing Recency

Truncating from the start means you keep the most recent content and drop the oldest content when you run out of space. Imagine you have a conversation that has grown to one hundred fifty thousand tokens, but your context window can only hold one hundred thousand tokens. Truncating from the start would mean you drop the first fifty thousand tokens of conversation history and keep everything from that point forward to the present.

This approach prioritizes recency over completeness. The underlying assumption is that what happened most recently is more relevant to understanding the current state of the conversation than what happened a long time ago. For many applications, this assumption holds true. In a customer support conversation, the issue being discussed right now matters far more than what was discussed ten minutes ago. In a coding assistant helping someone debug a problem, the most recent error messages and code changes are more important than the initial problem statement from twenty messages back.

The advantage of truncating from start is that the model always has access to the most recent context, which keeps the conversation feeling coherent and current. The user's last few messages and the assistant's last few responses are always present in full detail, so the model can respond appropriately to whatever is happening right now. The disadvantage is that you lose all early context completely. If a user mentioned their company name, specific requirements, budget constraints, or other important details in the first few messages of a fifty-message conversation, truncating from the start will make the model forget that information entirely. The model has no way to access what was dropped, and from its perspective, that earlier conversation simply never happened.

This strategy works well when you have external systems tracking important long-term information so the conversation context does not need to be the sole source of truth about what the user has said before. It also works when the nature of your application means that older context genuinely becomes irrelevant over time, such as real-time troubleshooting where each issue is independent, or simple question-answering where each query stands alone.

## Truncate From End: Keeping Historical Context

Truncating from the end is the opposite approach. You keep the oldest content and drop the most recent content when you run out of space. This means preserving the beginning of the conversation or document while sacrificing the end.

This strategy is far less common in practice because it creates a deeply confusing and frustrating user experience in conversational applications. Imagine having a conversation where the model remembers everything you said in the first ten minutes but has completely forgotten the last five minutes of discussion. From the user's perspective, the assistant appears to have amnesia about the current topic while inexplicably remembering ancient history. This violates user expectations so severely that you almost never want to do this in user-facing conversational systems.

The only scenarios where truncating from the end makes sense are specific document processing tasks where the beginning of a document is definitively more important than the conclusion. For example, if you are extracting metadata from legal contracts and the relevant information is always in the header section at the beginning of the document, you might truncate from the end to ensure you always capture that header even if it means losing the signature pages at the end. But even in these cases, you would typically use smarter strategies like extracting just the sections you need rather than blunt truncation.

For conversations and most real-world applications, truncating from the end is almost always the wrong choice and should generally be avoided unless you have a very specific and unusual use case that truly requires it.

## Smart Summarization: Compressing Instead of Discarding

Smart summarization takes a fundamentally different approach to the truncation problem. Instead of simply dropping tokens when you run out of space, you use the model itself to compress older content into a condensed summary before deciding what to keep in full and what to represent in compressed form. This is more sophisticated than either form of direct truncation and usually produces better results, though it comes with its own trade-offs.

The core idea works like this. When your conversation grows too large to fit in the available context window, you take the older portions of the conversation and ask the model to create a summary that captures the key facts, decisions, context, and important details. You then replace those older messages with this summary, which takes up far fewer tokens than the original messages. The remaining recent messages stay in their full, uncompressed form. This way you maintain both the detail of recent context and at least some compressed memory of what happened earlier in the conversation.

The advantage of smart summarization is that it preserves information that would otherwise be completely lost through direct truncation. If a user mentioned their budget constraints twenty messages ago, a good summary might include a line like "user has a budget of fifty thousand dollars for this project" even though the original detailed three-message discussion about budget trade-offs is no longer in the context. The model can still access that factual information through the summary, which means decisions made early in the conversation can still inform actions later on. This creates a much better user experience than truncation, where early context simply vanishes without a trace.

The disadvantage of smart summarization is that it requires an additional model call to generate the summary, which adds both latency and cost to your system. Every time you need to summarize, you are making an extra API request and paying for both the input tokens to feed the content being summarized and the output tokens for the generated summary. In high-volume applications, this can add up quickly. You are also at the mercy of what the summarization process decides to keep and discard. If the summarization step misses something important or introduces inaccuracies, those errors propagate forward into the rest of the conversation. A poorly designed summarization prompt might drop critical details or misrepresent what was actually said, and once that information is lost or corrupted in the summary, there is no way to recover it from the original messages.

Smart summarization makes sense when you need to maintain some awareness of earlier context but cannot afford to keep everything in full detail. This is appropriate for research assistants where early research findings should inform later synthesis, for complex multi-step workflows where decisions made at step two affect options at step seven, or for any application where the conversation builds on itself over time and losing early context would break the coherence of the interaction. The cost and latency overhead of summarization is justified when the quality improvement from maintaining historical context outweighs those costs.

## Hybrid Approaches: Combining Strategies in Production

In real production systems, these strategies are not mutually exclusive. The most sophisticated and effective implementations combine multiple truncation strategies in a layered approach that balances the trade-offs of each individual method.

A common pattern is to implement a three-tier memory structure. Messages from the last five turns are kept in full detail because they represent the current active context and need maximum fidelity. Messages from turns six through twenty are also kept in full but are marked as candidates for truncation from the start if space gets tight. This middle tier provides good detail for somewhat older context that is still relevant but not as critical as the most recent exchanges. Messages older than twenty turns are run through smart summarization to produce a compact summary that sits at the beginning of the context, providing compressed awareness of ancient history without consuming excessive tokens.

This hybrid approach gives you maximum detail for recent interactions where detail matters most, good detail for moderately old interactions that might still be relevant, and compressed awareness of very old context that provides continuity without overwhelming the context window. As new messages arrive, content flows through these tiers. Recent messages start in the full-detail tier, age into the middle tier, and eventually get summarized into the historical summary tier. Very old summaries might eventually be dropped entirely if even the compressed history becomes too large.

The key insight is that different parts of your conversation history have different value and different token efficiency. The last few messages are extremely high value and should be kept in full. Slightly older messages are moderate value and can be kept in full if space allows but truncated if necessary. Very old messages are low value in their full form but might contain important facts that justify the cost of summarization. By treating different age ranges differently, you can optimize your use of the available context window much more effectively than any single truncation strategy could achieve alone.

## Why This Matters

Understanding truncation strategies is essential because the choice you make shapes fundamental characteristics of your application. If you truncate from start aggressively, your system will always feel current but might forget important early decisions. If you rely heavily on summarization, your system will maintain better long-term coherence but at higher cost and latency. If you use naive truncation without considering what information matters most, you will see mysterious failures where the model seems to forget things users clearly told it or makes decisions that contradict earlier parts of the conversation.

This is not just about making things fit within technical constraints. It is about designing a memory architecture that matches your application's needs and your users' expectations. A customer support bot and a personal research assistant have fundamentally different memory requirements, and the truncation strategy you choose should reflect those differences. Getting this right means your application behaves predictably and maintains appropriate context across long interactions. Getting it wrong means frustrated users and unreliable behavior that is hard to debug and fix.
